# The Financial Wellness Order of Operations

*The canonical 14-step reference. This is the spine of AI2FI — every recommendation, goal, and module routing decision traces back here.*

---

## Why an *order* exists at all

Most financial advice is offered as a menu: open an IRA, max your HSA, pay down debt, invest in index funds, save for college. Every item is true. But served as a menu, it's nearly useless — because the same dollar produces wildly different outcomes depending on where you spend it.

A dollar put toward a 22% credit card before you've captured your employer match is a dollar that earned roughly −22% instead of +50% or more. Same dollar. Different order. Totally different life.

The Order of Operations isn't a ranking of importance. It's a sequence of *dependencies*. Each step earns the right to move to the next by being handled. Skip ahead and you build a tower on sand.

One more thing: the order is not a punishment. Step 14 is "Whatever You Want." The whole framework is a pathway to freedom, not a treadmill of discipline.

---

## The 14 Steps at a Glance

| # | Step | What it establishes |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | Track Income, Expenses, & Net Worth | Self-awareness — the precondition for everything |
| 2 | Appropriate Insurance Coverage | Protection against catastrophic reversal |
| 3 | Align Spending With Your Values | The *why* behind every other step |
| 4 | Starter Emergency Fund | A small buffer against ordinary surprise |
| 5 | Eliminate High-Interest Debt | Stop the bleeding before building |
| 6 | Employer Match | Free money, captured |
| 7 | Full Emergency Fund | A full buffer against significant surprise |
| 8 | Health Savings Account (HSA) | The best-tax-advantaged account that exists |
| 9 | IRAs (Roth or Traditional) | Tax-advantaged retirement space |
| 10 | Education Accounts | Tax-advantaged college space (if relevant) |
| 11 | Eliminate Non-Mortgage Debt | Finish paying off what still costs you |
| 12 | Max Retirement Account | Complete your tax-advantaged retirement capacity |
| 13a | Taxable Brokerage Account | Wealth accumulation beyond tax shelters |
| 13b | Mortgage Paydown Fund | Freedom from the biggest monthly obligation |
| 13c | Irregular Expense Fund | Funds for planned, infrequent costs |
| 14 | Whatever You Want! | You've earned it |

Steps 13a / 13b / 13c are *parallel*, not sequential — at that stage, the right mix depends on values and life stage, not algorithm.

## Step 1 — Track Income, Expenses, & Net Worth

**Goal:** You know, without guessing, what's coming in, what's going out, and what you own minus what you owe.

**Why here:** Everything downstream is a decision about money. You cannot decide well without data. Budgeting apps, spreadsheets, or a disciplined monthly review — the tool doesn't matter. The visibility does.

**Readiness check:** This is step 1. You're always ready.

**Common traps:**
- Tracking for a month, then stopping. Visibility has to be ongoing.
- Over-precision — categorizing every latte. Track at a useful level, not a perfect one.
- Waiting until "things settle down" — they won't.

**When you're done for now:** You can state your monthly take-home, your rough monthly outflow, and your current net worth within ten minutes of being asked. You revisit these numbers at least monthly.

---

## Step 2 — Appropriate Insurance Coverage

**Goal:** The catastrophes that would otherwise destroy your financial life are insured against at appropriate levels.

**Why here — before debt:** Because one uninsured catastrophe wipes out years of debt-paydown progress. Insurance is the structural integrity of the plan. Without it, every dollar saved is at risk.

**The big categories:**
- **Health insurance** — non-negotiable, even if only catastrophic
- **Auto insurance** — legally required in most states, plus liability protection
- **Homeowners or renters insurance** — protects your residence and liability exposure
- **Term life insurance** — if anyone depends on your income (spouse, kids)
- **Disability insurance** — often overlooked, yet statistically more likely to be needed than life insurance
- **Umbrella liability** — becomes relevant once net worth is meaningful

**Readiness check:** Step 1 is giving you real data, so you know what's actually at risk.

**Common traps:**
- Confusing "I have insurance through my employer" with "I have *appropriate* coverage." Review the actual policies.
- Over-insuring the small stuff (low-deductible plans, extended warranties) while under-insuring the catastrophic stuff (disability, umbrella).
- Treating insurance as an investment vehicle — it almost never is.

**When you're done for now:** You can name each policy you hold, its coverage limits, and why that level makes sense for your situation.

---

## Step 3 — Align Spending With Your Values

**Goal:** The money going out is going toward the life you actually want — not toward whatever default you inherited.

**Why here:** Before you ask "how do I save more?" you need to know *what you're saving for*. Spending aligned with values is sustainable. Spending that feels forced isn't. This step is the *why* that makes every subsequent step worth doing.

**Readiness check:** Step 1 shows you where your money goes. Step 2 ensures catastrophe won't force a misalignment. Now the question is — does the outflow reflect the life you're trying to build?

**Common traps:**
- Skipping this step to get to the "real" financial steps. This *is* the real financial step.
- Confusing values with categories. "I value family" and "I spend a lot on dining out with family" are not the same claim. Interrogate each.
- Austerity masquerading as values. Cutting every joy isn't alignment — it's depletion.

**When you're done for now:** You've named 3–5 values you want your money to serve, and you can point to specific line items in your spending that reflect each.

---

## Step 4 — Starter Emergency Fund

**Goal:** A starter reserve of immediately accessible cash, sized to your life situation — enough that ordinary surprises are non-events, but not so much that you over-solve before killing high-interest debt (step 5) or building the full fund (step 7).

**How much is "starter" for *you*?** Highly dictated by life situation. A rubric:

| Situation | Starter target |
|---|---|
| Dual income, no dependents | $1,000–$2,000 is usually enough to make a surprise a non-event |
| Dual income, with dependents | $2,000–$5,000, or roughly half a month's take-home |
| Single income, stay-at-home partner, no kids | ~1 month of salary |
| Single income, stay-at-home partner, 2+ kids | 1–3 months of salary |
| Self-employed or highly variable income | Lean toward 3 months even as a "starter" |

The pattern: the higher the concentration of risk (one earner, more dependents, less diversified income), the bigger the starter buffer needs to be before you go aggressive on debt. Otherwise, any ordinary hiccup — a medical copay, a busted transmission — becomes fresh credit-card debt that undoes your progress.

**A note on sequencing for higher-risk profiles:** If your starter target is 3 months of salary and your full-EF target (step 7) is 6 months, you'll reach the step 4 milestone while still carrying some high-interest debt. That's fine — it's the correct response to a high-concentration-of-risk life. Step 5 kicks in when your starter is funded; step 7 finishes the buffer after the debt is cleared.

**Why here — before killing high-interest debt:** Without a buffer, every surprise gets charged right back to the card you're trying to pay off. A starter fund keeps the debt payoff from turning into a treadmill.

**Readiness check:** You have visibility (step 1), insurance (step 2), and aligned spending (step 3) — so you can see where to redirect to build this quickly.

**Common traps:**
- Using a one-size-fits-all number. $2K is right for many households and nowhere near enough for others. Use the rubric above.
- Overbuilding past your situational starter before killing debt — the full emergency fund is step 7, not step 4. Don't over-solve here.
- Keeping it in a low-yield account where it's "invisible enough" to spend — use a named savings or high-yield account.
- Calling it an emergency fund and then using it for a new TV. Define *emergency* up front.

**When you're done for now:** Your situational starter amount sits in a named account you don't touch for non-emergencies.

---

## Step 5 — Eliminate High-Interest Debt

**Goal:** Credit cards, payday loans, and any debt at roughly 7%+ interest are paid off in full.

**Why here — before employer match:** This is the one exception to "grab free money first." Even the employer match's 50–100% match dollar-for-dollar can be outweighed by *compounding* credit card interest, and the psychological weight of high-interest debt distorts every other financial decision. Kill it fast, then move on.

**Readiness check:** Starter fund (step 4) is in place, so paying off debt won't immediately re-accumulate the moment life happens.

**Methods:**
- **Avalanche** (highest interest rate first) — mathematically optimal
- **Snowball** (smallest balance first) — psychologically reinforcing
- Either is fine. The best method is the one you actually execute.

**Common traps:**
- Paying minimums across many cards instead of concentrating fire.
- Closing cards after payoff out of excitement — can hurt credit utilization.
- Not changing the behavior that created the debt. Step 3 matters here.

**When you're done for now:** No outstanding balance above roughly 7% interest.

---

## Step 6 — Employer Match

**Goal:** You are contributing at least enough to your 401(k) (or equivalent) to capture the full employer match.

**Why here:** This is an instant 50–100% return on the matched portion. No other step in the sequence offers that. If you do nothing else for retirement, capture this.

**Readiness check:** High-interest debt is gone (step 5), so your match contributions aren't being silently offset by interest accrual elsewhere.

**Common traps:**
- Not understanding your match formula. "They match up to 6%" could mean several different things — know yours exactly.
- Leaving money on the table because of a vesting schedule you've given up on — reconsider in context.
- Picking the default target-date fund without understanding it (it's often fine; know why it's fine for you).

**When you're done for now:** You're contributing enough to capture 100% of your available match, and you understand the formula.

---

## Step 7 — Full Emergency Fund

**Goal:** 3–6 months of essential expenses in liquid, accessible cash (or cash-equivalent).

**Why here:** With the match captured, the next layer of resilience is a real buffer — enough to survive a job loss, medical event, or family crisis without destroying your progress.

**How much?**
- **3 months** — dual-income household, stable fields, strong network
- **6 months** — single-income household, variable income, specialized field
- **9–12 months** — self-employed, commissioned, or otherwise volatile

**Readiness check:** Match is captured (step 6); you're building on top of a structurally sound base.

**Common traps:**
- Parking the full fund in a 0.01% checking account — use a high-yield savings account.
- Investing the EF — it defeats the purpose if it drops 30% the same week you need it.
- Stopping before reaching the target because "I feel safe enough." The gap between "feels safe" and "is safe" is where unplanned debt lives.

**When you're done for now:** Your target months of essential expenses are sitting liquid and untouched.

## Step 8 — Health Savings Account (HSA)

**Goal:** Max your HSA contribution annually and — when life allows — use it as a long-term investment vehicle rather than a medical bill-pay account.

**Why here — before IRAs:** The HSA is the only **triple-tax-advantaged** account in the U.S. tax code:

- **Pre-tax contributions** — reduce taxable income today (like a Traditional 401(k))
- **Tax-free growth** — investments compound untaxed (like a Roth)
- **Tax-free withdrawals** — for qualified medical expenses (unique)

No other account combines all three. When eligible, prioritize it.

**Eligibility:** You must be on a qualified High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP). Without an HDHP, the HSA isn't available to you — skip to step 9. Limits for 2026: $4,300 individual / $8,550 family, plus a $1,000 catch-up at age 55+.

**Readiness check:** Full EF (step 7) means you can handle medical expenses out of pocket and let the HSA actually *grow* — which is where the real long-term power lives.

### The "Investment Vehicle + Receipt Bank" Strategy

Most people use their HSA wrong: as a passthrough that absorbs medical expenses as they happen. That works, but it wastes the account's real superpower.

The better play, for households that can afford it:

1. **Contribute to the HSA pre-tax.** Max it if possible.
2. **Invest the balance.** Don't leave it in cash. Most HSAs allow investing above a small cash threshold — move the rest into low-cost index funds or ETFs, just as you would in a brokerage. This is where the tax-free compounding happens.
3. **Pay current medical expenses out of pocket, in cash.** Not from the HSA. From regular checking/savings.
4. **Save every medical receipt.** Digital scans, organized by year. **There is no time limit** on when you can reimburse yourself — as long as the expense occurred after the HSA was opened.
5. **Years or decades later, reimburse yourself tax-free.** Your HSA has been growing tax-free the whole time. Your stack of receipts represents untaxed withdrawal capacity you can tap whenever you want.

**Why this works:** The HSA effectively becomes a Roth IRA that also covers current medical needs and decades of old receipts. On a pure tax-efficiency basis, it's the best account available to most Americans who qualify.

**The prerequisite:** You have to be able to pay current medical out of pocket without strain. If you can't, use the HSA normally for medical expenses as they come — you're still capturing the triple tax advantage, just not the investment compounding. Not every household is at a life stage where the receipt-bank strategy fits, and that's fine. It's worth revisiting when cash flow improves.

### After age 65

Non-medical withdrawals **before 65** incur a 20% penalty plus ordinary income tax — so early non-medical withdrawals are expensive. **After 65**, the penalty drops away and non-medical withdrawals are taxed like a Traditional IRA (ordinary income only). So at worst the HSA behaves like a Traditional IRA in retirement. At best, it's a tax-free reservoir of decades of invested gains.

**Common traps:**
- **Leaving the balance in cash.** The single biggest mistake. Most HSAs default to cash — you have to actively invest.
- **Using it for every current medical expense** when you could afford to pay out of pocket, forfeiting the long-term growth play.
- **Losing receipts.** Build a digital system from day one — a dated folder per year of scans or photos.
- **Forgetting the HSA when you change jobs.** Roll it to a provider with better investment options (Fidelity, Lively, etc.) if your employer's default is limited.
- **Confusing an HSA with a Flexible Spending Account (FSA).** FSA money is use-it-or-lose-it by year-end. HSA money rolls forever and is yours for life. Don't apply FSA thinking to HSA planning.

**When you're done for now:** Maxed at the annual limit, invested (not in cash), with a receipt-bank strategy in place if cash flow allows paying current medical out of pocket.

## Step 9 — IRAs (Roth or Traditional)

**Goal:** Max your IRA contribution annually.

**Why here:** After the HSA's unique advantage, IRAs are the next-best tax-advantaged retirement space, with contribution limits independent of your 401(k).

**Roth vs. Traditional (quick framing):**
- **Roth** — pay tax now, tax-free in retirement. Better when you expect to be in the same or higher bracket in retirement, or when you value tax diversification.
- **Traditional** — deduction now, tax in retirement. Better when you expect a meaningfully lower bracket in retirement.
- **Backdoor Roth** — for high-income households whose direct Roth contributions are phased out.

**Readiness check:** HSA is maxed (step 8) or not applicable.

**Common traps:**
- Contributing "someday" and missing annual limits (you can't retroactively add prior years).
- Leaving the contribution sitting in cash inside the IRA — you have to invest it.
- Not realizing you can contribute for the prior year until April 15 of the following year.

**When you're done for now:** Maxed at the annual limit, invested according to strategy.

## Step 10 — Education Accounts

**Goal:** Funding college (or other qualified education) for dependents through tax-advantaged vehicles — most commonly a **529 plan**, with **Coverdell ESAs** as a complementary tool for families who want investment flexibility.

**Why here:** Before pushing further into lower-priority debt and max retirement, families with education goals carve out a dedicated space for them — because time compounding matters most for 18-year horizons.

**This step is skippable** for users without education goals for dependents.

**Readiness check:** Your own retirement foundation (steps 6, 8, 9) is in place before funding someone else's future. Financial aid will help your kid with college. Nothing helps you with retirement.

### The two main vehicles

**529 Plan**

- **Contribution limits:** Very high — typically $300,000–$550,000+ per beneficiary, state-dependent. No annual cap, but contributions above the annual gift-tax exclusion require a gift-tax filing (or use of the 5-year forward-funding election).
- **State tax benefits:** Most states offer a deduction or credit for contributions to *your own state's* plan. Always check whether to use your home state's plan for the tax benefit vs. shopping nationally for lower fees or better investment options.
- **Qualified uses:** College tuition, room and board, books, required fees. Post-Tax Cuts and Jobs Act: up to $10,000/year for K-12 tuition. Post-SECURE Act: up to $10,000 lifetime per beneficiary for student loan repayment. Post-SECURE 2.0: limited rollovers of 529 to Roth IRA for the beneficiary, with caveats.
- **Investment menu:** Typically age-based glide-path funds plus a few static options. Limited, but adequate for most families.
- **Control:** Account owner retains control even after the beneficiary is an adult.
- **Beneficiary flexibility:** Change the beneficiary tax-free to another qualifying family member — useful if one child doesn't use all the funds.

**Coverdell ESA (Education Savings Account)**

- **Contribution limit:** $2,000/year *total* per beneficiary, across all contributors. Much lower than a 529.
- **Income limits:** Contribution phases out at higher modified AGI (~$95K–$110K single / ~$190K–$220K joint). High earners are effectively excluded, though workarounds exist (e.g., gifting to a lower-income contributor).
- **Qualified uses:** Same as 529, with broader historical treatment of K-12 expenses (uniforms, supplies, tutoring).
- **Investment menu:** Self-directed — pick any stock, ETF, or mutual fund, just like a brokerage. Much more flexible than a 529.
- **Control:** Transfers to the beneficiary at age 18 (vs. 529 owner retaining control for life).
- **Age limit:** Must be used by beneficiary age 30, or transferred to another family member.
- **State tax benefit:** Usually none.

### Why some families use both

A common pairing: the **529 as the primary pot** (for the state tax deduction and high contribution capacity), and a **Coverdell alongside** for the investment-flexible portion — letting the family hold individual stocks, specific ETFs, or investment themes the 529's menu won't allow. At $2K/year, the Coverdell will never be the bulk of education funding — but its investment autonomy is uniquely useful at this tier.

The right mix depends on three inputs: your state's 529 tax benefit (big deductions argue for 529-heavy), your income level (Coverdell eligibility), and whether you want to hand-pick investments vs. use a target-date default.

**Common traps:**
- Funding education at the expense of retirement. Put your own oxygen mask on first.
- Over-funding a 529 — balances well above education cost create withdrawal problems, though SECURE 2.0 added limited 529 → Roth conversion flexibility.
- Treating all college savings accounts as equivalent. State tax treatments, contribution limits, and investment menus vary substantially.
- Missing the Coverdell option because it was never mentioned. Income-eligible families who want control of their investments often benefit from holding both.
- Contributing to another state's 529 without checking whether your own state offers a deduction you're leaving on the table.

**When you're done for now:** On-pace to your education goal per child, funded by a sustainable monthly contribution rather than a lump sum you can't repeat.

## Step 11 — Eliminate Non-Mortgage Debt

**Goal:** Auto loans, student loans, personal loans, medical debt — all paid off.

**Why here:** High-interest debt was killed in step 5. What remains by this point is typically lower-interest, but still a drag on cash flow and psychological bandwidth. Clearing it frees up capacity for max retirement and wealth-building.

**Readiness check:** Retirement and education baselines are funded (steps 6, 8, 9, 10) — so you're not sacrificing tax-advantaged space to pay down 4% debt.

**A worthwhile question at this step:** Should this debt actually be paid off early, or just on schedule? For a 3% student loan, the arbitrage may favor investing the surplus. For a 7% auto loan, payoff wins. Run the math on each.

**Common traps:**
- Refinancing and then running up the original balance again.
- Paying off a very low-interest loan purely for the emotional relief (fine if you name it that, bad if you claim it's optimal).
- Ignoring the psychological cost of debt — some users genuinely function better without it, and that's a valid input.

**When you're done for now:** No non-mortgage debt outstanding, or a specific, funded payoff schedule you're on track with.

## Step 12 — Max Retirement Account

**Goal:** Max your 401(k) or equivalent to the annual limit — not just the match level — *up to the point where additional tax-deferred savings cost more flexibility than they earn.*

**Why here:** With HSAs, IRAs, education, and remaining debt handled, this is where you finish using your available tax-advantaged retirement space.

**Readiness check:** Steps 6–11 are in order. The match has been captured since step 6; this is about getting to the *full* annual limit — if and only if the mix still serves you.

### When maxing stops being optimal

There is such a thing as being *over-invested* in tax-advantaged retirement space. It's rare, but worth naming. Signs you may have crossed the line:

- **Early-retirement ambitions outstrip accessibility.** 401(k) and Traditional IRA funds are locked behind age 59½ penalties. If you want to retire at 55, a meaningful portion of your portfolio needs to be accessible without the 10% penalty — which means taxable brokerage (step 13a), Roth contributions (withdrawable anytime), or specific strategies like a 72(t) distribution or Roth conversion ladder. Over-funding Traditional accounts closes doors here.
- **Future RMDs are projected to push you into a higher bracket than today.** Traditional 401(k) and IRA balances that grow aggressively can create Required Minimum Distributions starting at age 73 that exceed your current marginal rate. "Defer now, pay less later" reverses when *later* is a bigger number than *now*.
- **Tax diversification is lopsided.** If nearly all your retirement assets are pre-tax, you're concentrated in a single tax regime. The future tax code is the single biggest unknown in retirement planning. A mix of Traditional, Roth, and taxable hedges that unknown.

At that point, the marginal dollar is better sent to **step 13a (taxable brokerage)** — full flexibility, long-term capital gains treatment, no age restrictions — than to further Traditional contributions.

### Rule of thumb

- **Match (step 6):** Always max.
- **HSA (step 8) and IRA (step 9):** Max almost always.
- **401(k) beyond match (step 12):** Max until your Traditional / Roth / taxable mix is reasonably balanced for your time horizon and planned retirement age. After that, route surplus to 13a.

**Common traps:**
- **Not using Mega Backdoor Roth** if your plan supports it (after-tax contributions + in-plan Roth conversion). One of the highest-leverage tools available, and most users don't know they have access.
- Failing to rebalance as balances grow.
- Not understanding the difference between **"maxing the match"** (step 6) and **"maxing the account"** (step 12). They're separate milestones.
- Reflexively maxing the 401(k) every year without checking whether the account-type mix still serves the plan. Step 12 isn't "always max" — it's "max until the math says 13a is better."

**When you're done for now:** Employee deferral limit maxed annually *or* a deliberate decision to redirect surplus to 13a because your mix has shifted.

## Step 13 — Parallel Options (a, b, c)

Once steps 1–12 are handled, the order loosens. The remaining goals are values-driven, and the right mix is personal.

### 13a — Taxable Brokerage Account

**Goal:** Wealth accumulation beyond tax-advantaged space.

**Why:** Flexibility — taxable accounts have no contribution limits, no age restrictions on withdrawals, and access to preferential long-term capital gains treatment.

**Common use:** Early retirement bridge (funding the gap before age 59½), major future purchases, legacy goals.

### 13b — Mortgage Paydown Fund

**Goal:** Accelerate payoff of the one remaining significant debt.

**Why:** The psychological and cash-flow freedom of a paid-off home is substantial, even when pure math would say to invest instead.

**Interest rate is the decisive input.** The right mix of 13a (invest) vs. 13b (pay down) depends heavily on your mortgage rate relative to expected market returns:

| Mortgage rate | Typical 13a ↔ 13b tilt |
|---|---|
| Below ~4% | Heavily 13a. Long-horizon market returns reliably exceed this rate, so prepayment is an expensive psychological comfort. |
| 4–5% | Lean 13a — roughly 70/30 or 80/20. |
| 5–6% | Roughly 50/50. Paydown is a risk-free return at your rate; market returns have variance. |
| Above 6% | Lean 13b. The risk-adjusted return of paydown is compelling. Also consider whether refinancing is available. |

This is a simplification — your tax bracket (if itemizing the mortgage interest deduction), time to retirement, and risk tolerance all matter — but rate is the dominant input.

**Common use:** Users with rates above 5% who value certainty, or users approaching retirement who want the cash-flow relief of a paid-off home.

### 13c — Irregular Expense Fund

**Goal:** Pre-funded cash for known-but-infrequent expenses — property tax, insurance premiums, car replacement, major appliance failures, roof replacement, medical deductibles, vet bills, home repair.

**Why:** Removes the "surprise" from expenses that aren't actually surprising. Prevents emergency fund raids for non-emergencies.

**Sizing it right, from your own data:** The best target comes from your own history, not a heuristic. If step 1 (tracking) has been running for a year or more:

1. Pull your actual irregular expenses over the past 1–3 years — not groceries or utilities, but the lumpy, non-monthly outflows.
2. Annualize the average.
3. Divide by 12 — that's your monthly 13c contribution target.

Three years of history makes this number dependable; one year is a starting estimate. If you don't have that history yet, use this as a reason to start tagging "irregular" expenses in your tracking now — the 13c conversation in a year will be dramatically sharper for it.

**Common use:** Homeowners, families with aging vehicles, anyone preparing for predictable lumpy expenses. If you've ever said *"every time I start to get ahead, something breaks"* — that's this fund's job.

**How to weight 13a/b/c:** Usually not equally. Mortgage rate dictates most of the 13a-vs-13b tilt (see the table above). Common patterns: heavy 13a with a modest 13c sinking fund when the mortgage rate is low; heavier 13b for near-retirees or anyone with a rate above 6%; balanced tri-split for high-earners eyeing early retirement.

---

## Step 14 — Whatever You Want!

**Goal:** Enjoy the life the first 13 steps made possible.

This isn't a rounding error. It's the point.

By the time you're consistently here, you've built resilience against catastrophe, insured your family, aligned your spending with what matters, eliminated debt, captured every tax advantage available to you, and still have surplus. Surplus is for *living*.

Step 14 can look like:
- An early-retirement date chosen, funded, and held to
- A second home, a sabbatical, a passion project, a business
- Substantial charitable giving beyond what's tax-optimized
- Paying for experiences that make your life meaningfully better

**The warning at step 14:** Accumulation is a habit. Some people never get out of it. Spending is a skill worth practicing. If step 14 feels uncomfortable, that's a signal to revisit step 3 — spending aligned with values — because the values *include the future you're funding*, not just the retirement you're deferring.

## How the system uses this document

The coaching layer uses this file as ground truth:

- **Part 1** ("Getting to know you") covertly maps each answer to the step it implies.
- **Part 2** (Walkthrough) renders these steps conversationally, with pause-points tied to the user's Part 1 answers.
- **Part 3** (Goal setting) walks each step and, based on comfort and competency data from Part 1, asks: *do you have a goal here? Are you ready for one? If not, what comes first?*
- **Quarterly check-ins** map the user's updated life against this document and flag which steps now need attention.

This framework is the spine. Everything else bends to it.
