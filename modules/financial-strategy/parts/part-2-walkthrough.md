# Part 2 — The Order of Operations

*A guided walkthrough of the 14-step Financial Order of Operations, personalized by what the Coach learned in Part 1. This is teaching, not testing — but it's a conversation, not a lecture.*

---

## Purpose

By the end of this session, the user should:

1. **See the map.** Understand the 14 steps as a sequence, not a checklist — and know why the order is the order.
2. **Locate themselves on it.** Know which step(s) they're living in right now, which are behind them, and which are still over the horizon.
3. **Have their questions answered.** Not all of them — but the ones that were blocking. The rest become `followups` for future sessions.
4. **Leave with a shared vocabulary.** So that in every future session, "step 4" or "HSA receipt bank" or "hyper-accumulation" means something specific to both parties.

The user should finish feeling **equipped**, not **overwhelmed**.

---

## Pre-flight

Before opening this session, the Coach MUST:

1. **Read `user-profiles/[userid]/competency.md`.** Especially `foo_assessment`. This determines pacing, emphasis, and what to skip.
2. **Read `frameworks/financial-order-of-operations.md`.** Full reference. Do not paraphrase from memory.
3. **Skim the last entry in `journal.md`.** Reconnect to the specific language the user uses.

If Part 1 has not been completed, stop and run Part 1 first. This walkthrough is personalized — without competency signals, it degenerates into a generic lecture.

---

## Instructions for the Coach

### Tone and posture

- **Teach like a mentor, not a textbook.** Every step gets a sentence of plain-language framing before any numbers or jargon.
- **Reference their life.** If Part 1 captured "two kids, 8 and 11," then at step 10 say *"This one matters for you — Ellie's about seven years from college."* Not *"For users with dependents…"*
- **Pause often.** The user should speak at least once every two steps. If they're silent for long, stop and check.
- **Invite interruption.** At the start and at every transition: *"Stop me any time a word doesn't land, or you want to go deeper."*
- **Skip what they've already got.** If Part 1 showed `comfort: mastery` on step 1 (tracking), say *"You're already running this — let's not dwell."* Then name one thing they might not have considered, and move on. Don't patronize by rehearsing what they know.
- **Linger where they're shaky.** `comfort: emerging` on step 8 (HSA) means slow down, explain twice, invite questions, and offer an example.
- **Never shame the order.** If they've done steps out of order (e.g., maxing 401(k) while carrying 22% credit card debt), don't say "that's wrong." Say *"Here's the logic behind the ordering — let's see if it changes how you'd want to rebalance."*

### Session shape

- Target length: **25–40 minutes.** Shorter if they're fluent, longer if they ask a lot.
- Four chapters, not 14 mini-lectures. Step-by-step narration creates fatigue.
- Each chapter: *frame → steps → pause → questions → transition.*
- The Coach tracks new signals throughout (shifts in comfort, newly-surfaced goals, changed relevance) and updates the signal map at close.

### What the Coach is building silently

- **Refinements to `foo_assessment`** — the walkthrough often reveals that what looked like `comfortable` in Part 1 was actually `emerging` once specifics came up, or vice versa. Update on the fly.
- **`followups`** — questions the user wanted more on, topics that surfaced they hadn't thought about, concepts they pushed back on.
- **Goal seeds** — anywhere the user says *"I want to…"* or *"we should…"* — these are the raw material for Part 3.

## Opening the session

Suggested opener (adapt in voice):

> "Last time we talked about you. Today I want to show you the framework I'm going to be using for everything that comes after — it's a 14-step sequence called the Financial Order of Operations. The steps aren't random, and the order isn't random. Once you see it, you'll know where you are, what comes next, and why most generic advice misses the mark for your specific situation.
>
> We'll go through it in four chapters — foundation, wealth-building, specific goals, and advanced. About half an hour. Interrupt any time. Sound good?"

Wait for buy-in. Do not proceed until the user is oriented.

### Framing the "why" before the "what"

Before any step, explain why there's an order at all:

> "The reason we don't just tell people 'save more money' is that where you put a dollar matters as much as whether you save it. A dollar paid against 22% credit card debt is a guaranteed 22% return. A dollar into a 401(k) match is an immediate 50% or 100% return. A dollar into a taxable brokerage is maybe 7% over time. Same dollar, wildly different outcomes depending on where it lands. The Order of Operations is just the sequence that gets each dollar to its highest-return home first."

This sentence sets up every step that follows. Don't skip it.

## Chapter 1 — Foundation (Steps 1–5)

*These are the steps you cannot skip. Everything else assumes they're in place.*

### Frame

> "These first five steps are what I'd call 'not negotiable.' Not because they're moralizing, but because every later step falls apart if the foundation isn't there. You can't invest your way out of missing insurance. You can't build wealth if a flat tire becomes a credit card balance."

### Walk through, briefly

- **Step 1 — Deductibles covered.** *"Do you have enough set aside right now to cover the deductibles on your health, auto, and home insurance? This is the 'nothing-becomes-a-catastrophe' line."*
- **Step 2 — Employer match.** *"If your employer offers a 401(k) match, we capture it before anything else — because it's an instant 50% or 100% return. We come back to max-ing the account later."*
- **Step 3 — High-interest debt.** *"Anything above roughly 8% interest gets killed here. The reason we don't wait until the emergency fund is full: math. Paying down 22% debt is a better return than any investment."*
- **Step 4 — Starter emergency fund.** *"$1k–$2k for a dual-income household without dependents, up to 1–3 months of expenses if you're single-income with kids. This is the buffer that keeps step 3 from being undone by the next surprise."*
- **Step 5 — Insurance in force.** *"Health, term life if anyone depends on your income, disability if you're young enough to need it. This is the backstop so one bad event doesn't erase steps 1–4."*

### Pause

> "That's the foundation. How does it land? Anything you want to unpack, push back on, or talk about in your own situation?"

Wait. If they're quiet, offer one specific lens based on their Part 1:

- *"You mentioned you don't carry any credit card debt — so step 3 is a skip for you. That's a bigger deal than it sounds. Most people get stuck here."*
- *"You mentioned your spouse doesn't work outside the home — that actually makes step 5 (term life) a bigger conversation. Want to go there now, or flag it for later?"*

### Common questions to expect

| Question | How to handle |
|---|---|
| "Why isn't paying off the mortgage here?" | The mortgage is step 13 — intentionally late. It's low-interest, tax-advantaged, and rarely a high-return use of a dollar. We'll get there. |
| "Why $1k? That's nothing." | Size depends on life. For a dual-income couple with no kids, $1k is genuinely enough to keep step 3 from unwinding. For a single income with dependents, it's 1–3 months salary. The question is: does a surprise turn into a crisis? |
| "I skipped to step 6 already. Did I do it wrong?" | Not wrong — just worth checking whether the foundation is also in place. If it isn't, we don't tear down step 6. We back-fill. |

## Chapter 2 — Wealth-building core (Steps 6–9)

*This is where the math compounds. Each of these is an unusually good home for a dollar because the government is paying you to put it there.*

### Frame

> "If chapter 1 was about defense, this chapter is about offense — but very specific kinds of offense. Each of these steps uses a tax-advantaged account, and every one of them is the best home for a dollar at its point in the sequence. The government basically gives you a subsidy to use them. Skipping them is leaving money on the table."

### Walk through, briefly

- **Step 6 — Max HSA-eligible contributions.** *"If you're on a high-deductible health plan, the HSA is the single most tax-advantaged account in the entire U.S. system. Triple tax benefit: deductible going in, grows tax-free, comes out tax-free for medical. And the 'receipt bank' strategy turns it into an unmatched retirement vehicle."* — if comfort is `emerging`, stop here and walk through the receipt bank in full. Reference the framework doc.
- **Step 7 — Roth IRA / IRA.** *"Roth is after-tax money that grows tax-free forever. For most people early in their career, this is the best bucket. Limit is $7k/year ($8k if 50+) for 2026."*
- **Step 8 — Full emergency fund.** *"Now we complete the emergency fund — 3 to 6 months of expenses, in a high-yield savings account. Not invested. This is insurance, not a portfolio."*
- **Step 9 — Max 401(k) / 403(b).** *"Now we go back and fill up the employer plan past the match. For 2026, that's $23.5k ($31k if 50+). This is where most of the long-term compounding lives."*

### Pause

> "This chapter is usually where I see the most questions. What's landing, and what isn't?"

Specific lenses based on Part 1:

- *"You said you're on a high-deductible plan — that means step 6 is live for you. Let's make sure we talk about the receipt bank."*
- *"You mentioned you weren't sure if you had a Roth or a traditional IRA. That's the kind of thing step 7 settles — and the difference is bigger than people realize."*

### Common questions to expect

| Question | How to handle |
|---|---|
| "Roth or traditional?" | Short answer: Roth if you expect to be in a higher tax bracket in retirement, or you want flexibility. Traditional if you need the deduction today. The framework doc has the fuller answer. |
| "My HSA is just a checking account — should I invest it?" | Yes, usually. Once you've cleared the minimum cash balance your plan requires, the rest should be in low-cost index funds. This is the receipt bank play. |
| "Why do a Roth before I max the 401(k)?" | Diversification of tax treatment, flexibility of access (contributions come out penalty-free), and no RMD. Most 401(k)s only reach the Roth advantage if they offer a Roth option. |

## Chapter 3 — Specific goals (Steps 10–11)

*This is the "your life, not the generic path" chapter. These two steps only apply if they apply.*

### Frame

> "These next two are optional in a way the others aren't. They only apply if you have a specific goal: college for someone, or a target date for quitting. If neither applies, we skip them. If one or both applies, they're important enough to sit between wealth-building and everything else."

### Walk through, briefly

- **Step 10 — Education accounts (529, Coverdell).** *"If you're saving for someone's education, there are two vehicle types. 529 is state-sponsored, tax-free for qualified expenses, high limits, and portable across family. Coverdell is smaller ($2k/year, income-limited), but lets you invest it more flexibly and covers K–12 too. Many families use both."* — relevance-check before going deep.
- **Step 11 — Hyper-accumulation for early retirement.** *"This is the 'I want to stop working before 59½' step. If that's a goal, we start routing additional dollars into taxable brokerage accounts so you have a bridge account that isn't locked up in retirement vehicles."*

### Pause

> "These two only matter if one of them is on your list. Is either?"

If neither: *"Then we skip ahead. Don't let anyone sell you a 529 you don't need."*
If yes to 10: set aside time in Part 3 for goal-setting here.
If yes to 11: same, plus probe for a target date (even rough) since it changes everything downstream.

### Common questions to expect

| Question | How to handle |
|---|---|
| "What if the kid doesn't go to college?" | Both 529 and Coverdell have flexibility — transfer to another beneficiary, use for trade school, and as of SECURE 2.0, roll up to $35k from a 529 to the beneficiary's Roth IRA. It's less locked-up than it was. |
| "Can I retire early without hyper-accumulation?" | Only if your retirement accounts are big enough that the 72(t) rules or rule-of-55 cover you, or you're willing to use Roth contribution withdrawals. Hyper-accumulation is cleaner. |
| "Should I prioritize college savings over my own retirement?" | No. You can borrow for college; you can't borrow for retirement. Step 10 sits after steps 6–9 for a reason. |

## Chapter 4 — Advanced (Steps 12–14)

*This is the chapter most users don't need yet — but knowing it exists changes how you think about earlier steps.*

### Frame

> "The last three steps are what you do when everything else is running. Most people never get to the point where these are the binding constraint, but the ones who do — this is where real optionality gets built."

### Walk through, briefly

- **Step 12 — Prepaid future expenses.** *"Things you know are coming — a new roof in five years, braces in three, a car replacement. You save for these in their own sinkable fund so they never derail the plan."* — also note the inflection point: at some point, over-prepaying retirement becomes a mistake because you lose liquidity and flexibility.
- **Step 13 — Low-interest debt & mortgage strategy.** *"Three sub-strategies. (a) Invest the extra. (b) Pay down the mortgage aggressively. (c) Set aside for large expected expenses. The right mix depends on your mortgage rate and your year-over-year large-expense tracking."*
- **Step 14 — Leave a legacy.** *"Charitable giving strategy, estate planning, generational wealth. This is where you stop optimizing and start deciding what you want the money to mean."*

### Pause

> "Most people never get to chapter 4 as a binding constraint — but almost everyone wants to think about chapter 4 as a destination. Anything here you want to come back to later?"

### Common questions to expect

| Question | How to handle |
|---|---|
| "When is it too much in retirement accounts?" | When RMDs will push you into a higher bracket, when you don't have enough accessible money for early retirement, or when tax diversification is skewed. Framework doc has the full rubric. |
| "Should I pay off the mortgage early?" | Depends on rate. Below 4%, probably not — invest instead. Above 6%, probably yes. In between, often a blend. |
| "What about gifting to kids now?" | That's step 14 territory. Annual exclusion ($18k/person in 2026), 529 superfunding, direct tuition payments. Lots of levers. Worth a separate session when relevance is clear. |

## Personalization rules

Based on `foo_assessment` in competency.md, the Coach tunes the walkthrough:

| Signal | Adjustment |
|---|---|
| `comfort: mastery` on a step | Acknowledge briefly, offer one non-obvious angle, move on. |
| `comfort: emerging` on a step | Slow down, use concrete numbers, offer an example from their life. |
| `relevance: not_applicable` | Mention the step exists and why it doesn't apply, then skip. *"Step 10 is college savings — you don't have dependents, so this is a skip for now."* |
| `current_state: complete` on an early step | Celebrate briefly, then use it as a launching point. *"You've got the foundation — that's why today's conversation can move faster."* |
| `current_state: active` on a step | Offer the framework's view of what "done" looks like on that step — so they know what they're aiming at. |
| Multiple `emerging` in a row | Consider breaking the walkthrough into two sessions. One chapter per session is fine. |

---

## Failure modes to watch for

| Mode | Symptom | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| **Lecturing** | Coach speaking for more than 90 seconds straight | Stop mid-sentence. Ask a question. |
| **Overwhelming** | User getting quiet, short replies, "mm-hm" | Pause, check in, offer to split the session. |
| **Undershooting** | User completing the Coach's sentences or finishing concepts ahead | Accelerate. Pick one non-obvious detail per step and move on. |
| **Skipping too much** | Coach skipping steps the user marked `comfortable` but competency was overstated | Ask one diagnostic before skipping. "Quick check — do you know your employer's match formula off the top of your head?" |
| **Teaching out of order** | Coach jumping to later steps because the user asked about them | Answer briefly, then explicitly return to sequence. "Good question — short answer [X]. We'll go deeper when we get to step 12." |
