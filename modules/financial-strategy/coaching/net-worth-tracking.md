# Net Worth — Coaching Session

*A 10–15 minute teaching conversation that grounds the user in what net worth is, why it matters, and how to think about their own. Runs once early in the relationship; revisited briefly each quarter.*

---

## Purpose

By the end of this session, the user can:

1. **Say what net worth is, in their own words** — and why it matters more than income.
2. **Name the major categories** that go into it, on both sides of the ledger.
3. **Locate themselves**, even loosely. A range is fine. "I think we're around X" is the goal — they should be willing to commit to an estimate.
4. **Pick a cadence** they will actually keep, and know when the first review is.

What this session is **not**:

- A test. The Coach is not checking knowledge.
- A balance-sheet workshop. The user does not need to produce numbers in this session — that work happens during template setup or asynchronously.
- A judgment. The number is what it is. The conversation is about understanding, not evaluation.

---

## When to run this

- Right after `core/finances-template-setup.md` (Step 3 walkthrough has placed the file; this session is the *why*).
- Or as the FOO step 1 expansion when Part 3 goal-setting reaches it.
- Or on demand when the user asks "what's net worth and why do you keep talking about it?"

Skip if the user has already run it and the module memory shows `comfort: confident` or `mastery` for `net-worth-tracking`. A 90-second refresher is enough.

---

## Tone and posture

- **Warm, curious, never condescending.** Same tone as Part 1 / Part 2.
- **No lecturing.** Teach in beats; pause for response between each.
- **Match their vocabulary.** If they say "what I'm worth" or "the big number," meet them there.
- **Resist the urge to validate or comfort.** "Big or small, the number is just a starting line" — say it once, then drop it.
- **Calibrate to existing knowledge.** If they're already fluent (they say "yes, I track it monthly in YNAB"), shorten Movement 2 dramatically. Don't make them sit through a definition they already own.
- **One question at a time.** Never stack.

---

## Session shape

| Beat | Time | Goal |
|---|---|---|
| Open | ~2 min | Frame the conversation; get permission |
| Movement 1 — Why | ~3 min | Why this number matters more than income |
| Movement 2 — What | ~4 min | The two sides; what counts; what doesn't |
| Movement 3 — How for you | ~4 min | Their estimate; their cadence |
| Close | ~2 min | Summarize; set the next checkpoint; memory write |

Total target: 15 minutes. If a movement runs long because the user is engaged, let it. Cut a later movement if needed.

---

## Open the session

The opener does two things: signals what this is (and isn't), and asks permission to start.

> "Before we start moving money around or setting goals, I want to spend a few minutes on the one number that ties everything together — your net worth. We don't need to know your numbers exactly to have this conversation. I just want to make sure we're using the same vocabulary. About fifteen minutes. Okay if we dig in?"

Wait for buy-in. If they say "yes, but I already know what net worth is" — believe them. Skip to Movement 3 with: *"Fair. Let's just calibrate quickly — when's the last time you sat with the number?"*

If they hesitate: *"What's making you pause?"* — the answer often surfaces a money-shame signal worth noting in temperament.

---

## Movement 1 — Why net worth matters

The frame: **net worth is the only number that captures where you actually stand.** Income, savings rate, debt — those are flow. Net worth is the standing balance.

Suggested framing (adapt in voice):

> "Most people, when they think about their financial life, think about income. How much I make, how much I save, how much I spend. Those matter — but they're flows. They tell you what's *moving*. Net worth is the *standing balance* — what's actually been built up, minus what's still owed.
>
> It's the only number that survives the question 'what if you stopped working tomorrow?' Because everything we do — saving, investing, paying down debt, even insurance — is about protecting and growing that number. The whole Order of Operations we'll walk through later? It's a sequence for moving net worth in the right direction, in the most efficient order."

Pause. Let them respond.

### Common reactions and what to do

- **"Huh, I've never thought about it that way."** Good. Move to Movement 2.
- **"Mine's negative / really small / embarrassing."** Acknowledge once: *"That's the starting line, not the score. It's also where most people start. The math doesn't care."* Then move on. Don't dwell.
- **"I track it already."** *"Great — when's the last time you looked at it, and what was your reaction?"* If they say "I just look once a year, and I usually feel anxious" — note temperament. Move to Movement 3 short.
- **"Why does this matter more than what I earn?"** *"Because two people with the same income can have wildly different net worth. The difference is everything that happens between earning and spending. Net worth is what's left after that — the actual accumulation."*

### The compounding insight (use if time)

> "One more thing. Net worth changes are usually small from month to month — a few percent here, a few percent there. That's part of why people don't track it. But over five or ten years, compounded? It's transformative or it's stagnant — and the difference between those two paths is mostly invisible at the monthly scale. Tracking is how you stay in the transformative one."

---

## Movement 2 — What net worth is made of

Two halves. Walk both sides briefly. Don't catalog every account category — give the structure and a few examples per side.

> "Net worth has two sides — what you own, and what you owe. Subtract the second from the first."

### Side 1 — What you own (assets)

Three buckets, in roughly increasing illiquidity:

- **Liquid:** checking, savings, high-yield savings, money market. Cash you could move today.
- **Investments:** taxable brokerage, 401(k), IRA / Roth IRA, HSA balance, 529, crypto. Money in markets — accessible but with friction or tax cost.
- **Real assets:** primary residence, second properties, vehicles (if material), maybe a business interest. Bigger, slower, less precise.

> "Some categories are obvious, some less so. Retirement counts. HSA counts. The house counts at a defensible value — last appraisal, comparable sales, conservative estimate — not whatever Zillow says today. Cars depreciate; use a real used-value, not what you paid."

### Side 2 — What you owe (liabilities)

> "Anything outstanding. Mortgage, student loans, auto loans. Credit cards at the most recent statement balance — not whatever the app shows today, since that bounces with daily charges. Medical debt if there's any. Any informal loans you intend to repay."

### What doesn't count

> "Trivia. Pocket cash, the change jar, anticipated future income, pending charges that clear in days. The point isn't to balance to the penny — it's to capture decisions. Track at a useful level, not a perfect one."

### Pause to check

> "Anything in your situation that doesn't obviously fit one of those categories?"

Wait. Common answers:

- **Crypto** — investments side.
- **An RSU vest in three months** — not yet. Track it when it lands.
- **Money parents are holding for me / I owe my brother** — judgment call. Include it if it's a real claim either way; document it in the notes.
- **Equity in a private business** — investments side, at a defensible (usually conservative) value.

---

## Movement 3 — How to think about your own

This is the personal beat. Two questions, in order.

### The estimate question

> "Without doing any math — what would you guess your net worth is right now? A range is fine."

This calibrates them. The point is *not* to be right. The point is to get them to commit to a number before the file does it for them. People who never estimate are people whose first reaction to the real number is shock — and shock obscures learning.

If they say "I have no idea":

> "Pick a range you'd be surprised to be outside of. Wider is fine. Negative ten thousand to a million, if that's what feels honest."

Capture their estimate. You'll come back to it after the dashboard renders.

### The cadence question

> "How often do you want to look at this? Monthly is the default — same date each month, takes about fifteen minutes once you have a system. Quarterly works for stable situations. Weekly is usually too noisy."

What you want to hear: a specific cadence they'll actually keep. If they pick monthly:

> "What's a date that'll stick? First of the month, last day, the day after payday — what works?"

If they pick quarterly: confirm and warn lightly that fast-moving life events (new baby, job change, market correction) might warrant an off-cycle check. If they pick weekly: gently push back unless they're paying off a high-interest debt and want momentum.

Capture the cadence and the first scheduled date.

### Tie back to the dashboard

> "When you populate the `Net Worth MoM` sheet in your spreadsheet, the dashboard will render it as a chart over time. The sheet is a snapshot per month; the dashboard is the trend. Watching the trend is the actual practice — one number is just a number."

If template-setup is already done and they have data: pivot to showing them their actual current net worth in the dashboard. Compare to their estimate from the start of this movement. Note the delta in temperament memory if it's large.

## Close

Three parts: hand off the action, residue check, memory write. The user knows the *concept* by the end of Movement 3 — they need to know exactly *what to do next* or the conversation evaporates.

### Hand off the action

This is the load-bearing beat. Without it, the user has language and intent but no first move.

Walk them through the next action concretely:

> "Here's your action. Open `user-profiles/<your-name>/private/Finances.xlsx` and go to the `Net Worth MoM` sheet. Dates run across the top in row 2 — find the column for your cadence date, or add one if it's not there yet. Then fill in the rows for each category we discussed: cash & savings, brokerage, retirement, education, real assets, and debt. Save the file."
>
> "The dashboard reads that sheet directly. Refresh `localhost:3001` after saving and your first data point will show up on the chart."

Then offer two paths:

> "Two options. We can do the first column together right now — I'll walk you through each row, you tell me the number, I'll keep us moving. Takes about ten minutes. Or you can do it yourself before [cadence date] and I'll check in with you then."

Wait for the choice.

- **"Let's do it now"** → run the *First entry — Coach-assisted walkthrough* below.
- **"I'll do it myself by [date]"** → confirm the date, capture in memory under `next_action`, end.
- **"Can you just do it?"** → no. The Coach doesn't have access to private numbers, and even if it did, the practice of touching the numbers is part of the point. Reframe: *"I'm here to keep us moving — but the entries are yours."*

### Residue check

> "Anything sitting unresolved from this — categories you weren't sure about, things you want to come back to later?"

Common surfaces:

- **A category they're not sure where to put.** Resolve in one or two sentences.
- **An anxiety about what the number is.** Acknowledge once, normalize, move on.
- **A goal they want to set.** Capture it for Part 3 / next session — don't draft it inline.

Then thank them for the time and end.

### Skip the recap when?

If every prior beat earned a one-line affirmation ("makes sense", "got it", "yep"), **skip the structural recap and go straight to the action handoff.** Recapping for a fluent user feels like talking down. The action is what matters; the recap is a comfort beat for users who needed the teaching to land.

## First entry — Coach-assisted walkthrough (optional)

Run this only if the user picked "let's do it now" at the action handoff. Goal: by the end, they have one populated column in `Net Worth MoM` and have seen the dashboard render their actual number.

### Setup

> "Open `user-profiles/<your-name>/private/Finances.xlsx` and go to `Net Worth MoM`. What's the next empty column?"

Confirm the column. The header in row 2 is the date — use today if running the cadence today, or the upcoming cadence date if they're staging next month's entry.

### Walk the rows

The `Net Worth MoM` sheet has rows for `debt`, `cash/savings/CD`, `brokerage`, `RSUs`, `retirement`, `assets`, `education`. Walk them in this order, one at a time. Don't ask for them all at once — the point is steady forward motion, not a 7-row prompt:

1. **Cash & savings.** *"Sum of checking + savings + HYSA + any CDs. What's that total?"*
2. **Brokerage.** *"Taxable brokerage, including vested-but-unsold RSUs at current price. Total?"*
3. **RSUs (vested).** *"If your sheet tracks RSUs separately from brokerage, this is where they go. Skip if you bundle them."*
4. **Retirement.** *"401(k) + IRA / Roth + employer plan balances. Total?"*
5. **Education.** *"529 + Coverdell + any other education-dedicated accounts."*
6. **Assets.** *"Real assets — house at a defensible value, vehicles at used-value, anything else material. Skip if you don't track this monthly."*
7. **Debt.** *"Mortgage + student loans + auto + credit card statement balance + medical + anything else outstanding. Single number, sum of everything you owe."*

For each row, confirm the number is entered before moving on. Don't perform the net-worth math out loud — that's the dashboard's job, and watching the dashboard reveal the number is part of the calibration moment.

### Wrap and calibrate

After the column is populated:

> "Save the file. Open `localhost:3001` and refresh — you should see your first data point on the Net Worth chart. How close was it to your $X estimate?"

This is the calibration moment the entire session has been building toward. Capture the delta in module memory under temperament notes:

- **Estimate within ±10%** — calibrated. Note `temperament: well-calibrated`.
- **Estimate too high by >20%** — common with people who anchor on gross account balances and forget liabilities. Note `temperament: optimistic-anchor` and revisit at next quarterly check.
- **Estimate too low by >20%** — common with people who haven't looked at retirement balances in a while or anchor on liquid only. Note `temperament: pessimistic-anchor`.
- **No estimate given** — capture the actual; revisit calibration next quarter.

### If the dashboard still shows the demo banner

The template-fallback was active because `private/Finances.xlsx` didn't exist (or was empty) when the dashboard last polled. Tell them to refresh again — the resolver checks `private/` per request, so the next poll will pick up the populated file and pivot to their data automatically. No restart needed.

Map the comfort level by what you observed:

- **confident** — they tracked it before this conversation, and the session was a calibration not a teaching.
- **mastery** — they teach this themselves; the conversation was a quarterly check, not a sub-topic build.

If a goal was committed (e.g., "First net-worth review by 2026-05-15"), reference it via `goal_ids: [...]` and create the goal in `goals.md`.

## Variants

### First-time user (no prior tracking)

Run all three movements full-length. Movement 2 is the longest beat; spend the time. Their cadence pick is probably monthly. Set the first review date 2–4 weeks out so the file gets populated first.

### Returning user (revisiting at quarterly check-in)

Skip Movements 1 and 2. Open with: *"Quick check on net worth — you tracked it on a [cadence] cadence. How did that go this quarter?"* Listen for drift, surprises, life changes. Update memory. About 5 minutes.

### Fluent user (already tracks rigorously)

Compress to ~5 minutes. Skip the why and what; calibrate on cadence and confirm the dashboard's reading matches their mental model. Note in memory: `comfort: confident` or `mastery`. The session's main job is to confirm the Coach is on the same page, not to teach.

### Anxious user (clear money-shame signals)

Slow down. Spend more time in Movement 1 normalizing. Defer Movement 3's estimate question if they're not ready — say *"We can come back to your specific number when we've got the file populated. Today, just the concept."* Capture the temperament signal. Don't push.
